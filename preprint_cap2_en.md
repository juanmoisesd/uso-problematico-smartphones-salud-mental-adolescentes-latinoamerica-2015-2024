# The Geography of Calculation: Mapping the Functional Neuroanatomy of Mathematical Processing

**Abstract**
The human capacity for mathematical reasoning is supported by a complex and distributed network of brain regions. This paper provides a comprehensive review of the functional neuroanatomy involved in calculation, moving beyond the traditional view of a single "math center." By synthesizing evidence from functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI), Positron Emission Tomography (PET), and lesion studies, we map the roles of the bilateral intraparietal sulcus (IPS) in magnitude processing, the prefrontal cortex (PFC) in executive control and working memory, and the ventral temporal-occipital areas in digit recognition. We further examine the white matter connectivity—specifically the superior longitudinal fasciculus—that integrates these nodes into a functional "math circuit." The discussion highlights individual differences in brain activation patterns and the impact of mathematical expertise on structural neuroplasticity. This mapping is essential for understanding both typical mathematical development and the neural basis of atypical conditions such as developmental dyscalculia.

**Keywords:** Neuroanatomy, fMRI, Intraparietal Sulcus, Prefrontal Cortex, Neural Networks, Mathematical Cognition, Connectivity.

## Introduction: The Multidimensional Nature of the Mathematical Brain
Where do numbers reside in the brain? This question has fascinated scientists since the early days of neuropsychology, when Gerstmann (1940) identified a cluster of symptoms, including acalculia, following damage to the left angular gyrus. Modern neuroimaging has refined this "geography of calculation," revealing that mathematical thought is not the product of an isolated module but emerges from the synchronized activity of a global network. While the "number instinct" discussed in our previous work (De la Serna, 2024a) provides the biological foundation for quantity perception, the transition to formal calculation requires the recruitment of additional cortical areas dedicated to symbolic processing, rule maintenance, and spatial reasoning.

This paper aims to provide a detailed "roadmap" of the mathematical brain, exploring the hierarchical and parallel processing streams that allow a human being to move from the simple perception of a set of objects to the resolution of high-level differential equations. We will examine the specialized roles of the parietal, frontal, and temporal lobes, as well as the subcortical structures and white matter tracts that bind them into a cohesive functional unit.

## Method: A Systematic Review of Neuroimaging Evidence
A systematic review of neuroimaging literature was conducted, focusing on studies that utilized task-based fMRI, PET, and DTI to isolate calculation-related activity. We categorized findings into three primary domains: (1) Core magnitude processing, (2) Symbolic and linguistic retrieval, and (3) Executive and attentional control. Research involving high-resolution structural MRI was also included to evaluate the role of brain connectivity and structural plasticity. All cited studies were selected based on their methodological rigor, the size of their participant cohorts, and the presence of direct neuroanatomical evidence for mathematical processing. We also reviewed meta-analyses of over 100 fMRI studies to identify the most consistent nodes of activation across diverse mathematical tasks.

## Results: The Cortical Landscape of Mathematics
### 1. The Parietal Hub: The Intraparietal Sulcus (IPS)
The bilateral intraparietal sulcus is the most consistently activated region during numerical tasks. Functional MRI studies show that the IPS acts as a "digital accumulator," with activity increasing as the numerical distance between two numbers decreases (the distance effect). This area is involved in both non-symbolic magnitude comparison and symbolic calculation (Dehaene et al., 2003). The IPS is organized topographically, with different populations of neurons responding to different quantities, functioning as a mental "number line."

Furthermore, the posterior parietal cortex (PPC) is involved in the spatial manipulation of numbers. When we perform mental arithmetic, we often move our "internal attention" along this mental number line, a process that recruits many of the same circuits used for eye movements and spatial navigation. This explains why mathematical and spatial abilities are so deeply intertwined.

### 2. The Frontal Executive: Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)
While the parietal lobe processes the "what" of number, the frontal lobe—particularly the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)—manages the "how." The PFC is essential for maintaining the rules of a mathematical operation, monitoring progress, and managing the high demands on working memory during multi-step problems (Arsalidou & Taylor, 2011).

In children, PFC activation is often higher than in adults, suggesting that as mathematical skills become automated, the brain shifts from frontal executive control to more efficient parietal retrieval (Rivera et al., 2005). The frontal lobe also plays a crucial role in "inhibitory control," allowing the individual to suppress intuitive but incorrect answers in favor of logical procedures.

### 3. The Visual-Symbolic Interface: The Ventral Stream
The recognition of Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3) recruits the "number form area" (NFA) located in the ventral temporal-occipital cortex, specifically the right fusiform gyrus (Schrouff et al., 2016). This area specializes in the visual processing of digits and must communicate effectively with the IPS to assign numerical value to these symbols. Disruption of the connectivity between the NFA and the IPS is a key feature of certain types of dyscalculia, where patients can see the numbers but cannot understand their magnitude.

### 4. Integration and Connectivity: The White Matter Scaffold
The efficiency of mathematical processing depends on the integrity of white matter tracts. The superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF) and the arcuate fasciculus provide the high-speed "highways" that connect the parietal, frontal, and temporal nodes. DTI studies have shown that the fractional anisotropy (a measure of white matter integrity) in these tracts predicts mathematical performance in both children and adults (Tsang et al., 2009). The "strength" of these connections is often a better predictor of mathematical success than the volume of any single grey matter area.

## Discussion: The Triple Code Model and Expertise
Mapping the geography of calculation reveals a highly specialized yet flexible system. The "Triple Code Model" (Dehaene & Cohen, 1995) remains the most robust framework for understanding these findings, proposing that numbers are represented in three formats: a visual-symbolic code (fusiform gyrus), a verbal code (angular gyrus), and an analog magnitude code (IPS).

One significant finding is the role of neuroplasticity. Mathematical experts often show increased grey matter density in the IPS and PFC, along with more efficient metabolic processing (Popescu et al., 2016). In expert calculators, the brain often recruits additional areas, such as the parahippocampal gyrus, to store intermediate results, effectively expanding the limits of working memory. Conversely, developmental dyscalculia is associated with reduced connectivity and structural abnormalities in the IPS, confirming that the "geography" of the brain is a critical determinant of mathematical ability.

## Future Directions: Personalized Neuro-Education
Future research should prioritize the use of longitudinal "connectome" mapping to track how mathematical networks evolve from childhood through adulthood. We need to understand how different instructional methods (e.g., rote vs. conceptual) shape the connectivity of the math circuit. Furthermore, the application of non-invasive brain stimulation (such as tDCS) to target specific nodes in the math circuit holds promise for remediating learning disabilities. Understanding the real-time dynamics of these networks through combined EEG-fMRI will also be vital for capturing the rapid transitions between intuition and analytical logic during problem-solving.

## Conclusions
The "brain for math" is a distributed symphony of specialized areas. The parietal cortex provides the semantic core, the frontal lobe provides the executive framework, and the ventral stream provides the symbolic interface. The seamless integration of these areas via robust white matter connectivity is what allows for the extraordinary human capacity for abstract calculation. By mapping this geography, we move closer to personalized educational interventions that respect the unique neural landscape of every learner. Mathematical proficiency is not just about "talent," but about the health and connectivity of this complex global network.
