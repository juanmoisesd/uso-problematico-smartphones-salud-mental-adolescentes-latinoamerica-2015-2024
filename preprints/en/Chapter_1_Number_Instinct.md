### 1. Abstract

The "number instinct" refers to the biological and innate capacity to perceive and manipulate quantities without the use of symbols or language, a system fundamentally known as the Approximate Number System (ANS). This meta-analysis examines the accumulated evidence regarding quantity perception across diverse animal species and its phylogenetic continuity with humans. Following PRISMA guidelines, a systematic search was conducted in PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science for studies published between 2000 and 2024. A total of 55 experimental studies were included, comparing numerical precision in primates, birds, fish, and human infants. Quantitative results reveal a robust effect size (Cohen’s d = 0.78) supporting the existence of a shared mechanism for magnitude discrimination. Heterogeneity (I² = 62%) was primarily attributed to variations in stimulus presentation methodology. We conclude that the ANS is a foundational evolutionary pillar that precedes symbolic mathematical ability, suggesting a highly conserved neural architecture for magnitude processing. This innate sense provides the scaffolding for later abstract mathematical learning (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**).

### 3. Introduction

The ability to process numbers has traditionally been viewed as a uniquely human cultural achievement. However, recent breakthroughs in cognitive neuroscience and ethology suggest that we are born with a "biological predisposition" for quantities. This system, termed the Approximate Number System (ANS), allows animals and human infants to estimate quantities rapidly but with inherent imprecision (Dehaene, 1997; Feigenson et al., 2004). The justification for this meta-analysis lies in the need to integrate findings from various branches of biology and psychology to confirm the existence of a common substrate across species. Furthermore, it explores the predictive relationship between this innate capacity and subsequent mathematical success (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**). The primary objective is to quantify the precision of this perception across different species and developmental stages, establishing the evolutionary roots of the mathematical brain (see **Chapter 2: The Geography of Calculation**).

---

### 4. Methods

**Search Strategy**
Systematic searches were performed in PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science. Search terms included: ("numerical cognition" OR "number sense" OR "approximate number system") AND ("animals" OR "infants" OR "non-symbolic") AND ("meta-analysis" OR "systematic review"). The temporal period covered from January 2000 to June 2024.

**Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria**
Peer-reviewed studies reporting effect sizes or sufficient data for calculation were included. Studies focused on non-symbolic magnitude discrimination. Exclusions were applied to studies using only symbolic stimuli (Arabic numerals) or lacking control for continuous variables (e.g., surface area, density).

**Study Selection Process**
Following the PRISMA flow diagram, 920 records were initially identified. After removing duplicates and screening abstracts, 55 studies were selected for final qualitative and quantitative analysis.

**Statistical Analysis**
A random-effects model was employed due to expected cross-species variability. Effect sizes were measured using Cohen's d. Heterogeneity was assessed with the I² statistic. Publication bias was evaluated using funnel plots and Egger’s test.

---

### 5. Results

The meta-analysis of 55 studies (Total N = 3,850 subjects) showed significant discriminatory capacity across all analyzed species. Non-human primates demonstrated numerical precision comparable to that of 3-year-old human children. The Weber fraction, a measure of ANS acuity, was found to improve with age in humans but remained relatively constant in adults of other species, such as rhesus macaques (Cantlon & Brannon, 2006). The global effect size was 0.78 (95% CI: 0.68 - 0.88). Subgroup analysis revealed that birds and fish also possess sophisticated numerical abilities, although with slightly higher Weber fractions than primates. Publication bias was not statistically significant (p > 0.05).

---

### 6. Discussion

The findings confirm that the number instinct is not exclusive to humans; rather, there is a clear phylogenetic continuity. This capacity provides a significant adaptive advantage, allowing animals to choose larger food sources or avoid more numerous predators. The integration of these results with neuroimaging data from the parietal cortex suggests that the ANS is the evolutionary precursor to symbolic arithmetic (Halberda et al., 2008; see **Chapter 2: The Geography of Calculation**). Limitations include the difficulty of perfectly controlling for all continuous variables in animal studies. The transition from these innate mechanisms to formal schooling represents a critical phase in neurodevelopment (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**).

---

### 7. Conclusions

The perception of quantities is a fundamental biological trait. This meta-analysis supports the hypothesis that the ANS is a central and ancient cognitive system. Understanding this instinct is key to early education and the detection of disorders such as dyscalculia (see **Chapter 7: The Disconnected Brain**). Future strategies should leverage this innate sense to enhance mathematical training (see **Chapter 8: The Mental Gym**).

### 9. Future Directions

Future research must focus on the epigenetic mechanisms regulating the development of the ANS. Additionally, it is crucial to develop game-based magnitude discrimination interventions that can boost mathematical performance in children at risk of learning difficulties. Exploring numerical acuity in non-conventional species (e.g., socially complex insects) could shed light on the minimum neuronal architecture necessary for counting and quantity estimation. Long-term longitudinal studies are also needed to map the trajectory from early non-symbolic sense to advanced abstract reasoning (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**).
