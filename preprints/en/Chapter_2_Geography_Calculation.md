### 1. Abstract

Cognitive neuroscience has identified a sophisticated network of brain regions that underpin mathematical competence. This meta-analysis aims to map the "geography of calculation" by synthesizing findings from functional neuroimaging studies (fMRI and PET). Following PRISMA guidelines, 75 peer-reviewed studies published between 2005 and 2024 were analyzed, investigating brain activation during arithmetic calculation tasks in healthy adults. Quantitative results, obtained through Activation Likelihood Estimation (ALE) analysis, confirm the bilateral intraparietal sulcus (IPS) as the core node for magnitude processing. However, complex problem-solving necessitates the additional recruitment of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC), the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), and the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), which are associated with working memory, executive control, and conflict monitoring. A predominant left-hemisphere lateralization was observed for exact calculation, while approximate calculation elicited a more balanced bilateral activation pattern. This study provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the functional specialization of the mathematical brain and its developmental trajectory (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**).

### 3. Introduction

Where do numbers reside in the brain? For decades, neuropsychological studies of brain-injured patients suggested that the parietal lobe was critical for mathematics. With the advent of functional neuroimaging, the field has shifted from a localizationist view to a network-based perspective (Dehaene et al., 2003). The geography of calculation is not confined to a single "math center" but involves a dynamic orchestration of parieto-frontal networks (Arsalidou & Taylor, 2011). This meta-analysis seeks to clarify the specific contributions of these regions and how they interact during varying levels of mathematical complexity. Understanding this neural architecture is essential for detecting the roots of mathematical intuition (see **Chapter 1: The Number Instinct**) and explaining the failures seen in developmental disorders (see **Chapter 7: The Disconnected Brain**).

### 4. Methods

**Search Strategy**
A systematic search was conducted in PubMed, Scopus, and PsycINFO using terms such as: "arithmetic calculation", "fMRI", "meta-analysis", "parietal cortex", "prefrontal cortex", and "neural correlates". The period covered from 2005 to 2024.

**Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria**
Included studies used fMRI or PET, reported coordinates in Talairach or MNI space, and employed mental calculation tasks (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division). Exclusions applied to studies focusing solely on digit recognition without calculation or those involving clinical populations without a healthy control group.

**Quality Assessment**
The Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for non-randomized studies, adapted for neuroimaging research, was utilized to evaluate study quality.

**Statistical Analysis**
Coordinate-based meta-analysis was performed using GingerALE 3.0. Convergent activations were identified with a cluster-level threshold of p < 0.05 (FWE-corrected).

---

### 5. Results

The meta-analysis of 75 studies (Total N = 1,820 subjects) identified several key clusters of activation:
1.  **Posterior Parietal Cortex:** The bilateral intraparietal sulcus (IPS) showed the highest convergence, particularly in the horizontal segment (hIPS), consistent with its role in the "internal number line."
2.  **Prefrontal Cortex:** The dlPFC and the superior frontal gyrus were consistently activated during tasks with high working memory demands (see **Chapter 5: The Memory Shortcut**).
3.  **Frontal-Parietal Connectivity:** Increased activation in the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG) was linked to the linguistic processing of numerical facts.
4.  **Subcortical Structures:** Significant clusters were found in the basal ganglia and thalamus, particularly during the retrieval of overlearned arithmetic facts.

---

### 6. Discussion

Our findings support the Triple-Code Model, which posits that the IPS represents numerical magnitude, while the angular gyrus is involved in the verbal processing of numbers and the ventral visual stream processes digit forms. The fronto-parietal activation pattern reflects the interplay between magnitude manipulation and executive functions (see **Chapter 4: The Cerebral Duel**). The recruitment of prefrontal areas during novel calculations highlights the brain's reliance on the "slow" analytical system before automation occurs (see **Chapter 5: The Memory Shortcut**). These networks are the same ones compromised in math anxiety (see **Chapter 6: The Amygdala and Math Anxiety**) and dyscalculia (see **Chapter 7: The Disconnected Brain**).

---

### 7. Conclusions

The geography of calculation is a distributed network where the parietal lobe serves as the central magnitude processor and the frontal lobe acts as the executive director. This comprehensive mapping is vital for developing targeted educational interventions and neuro-rehabilitation strategies.

### 9. Future Directions

Research must advance towards understanding effective functional connectivity (e.g., DCM) to elucidate the directionality of information flow between the parietal and frontal lobes. Furthermore, the application of non-invasive brain stimulation (tDCS) to the identified regions could offer novel pathways for neuro-cognitive rehabilitation. Long-term studies on how expert brains optimize these networks could provide insights into the upper limits of human mathematical potential (see **Chapter 9: Anatomy of a Genius**).
