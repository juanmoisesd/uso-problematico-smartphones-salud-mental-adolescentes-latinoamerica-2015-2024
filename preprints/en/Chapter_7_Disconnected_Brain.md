### 1. Abstract

Developmental dyscalculia (DD) is a specific learning disorder that impairs the acquisition of arithmetic skills, with an estimated prevalence of 3% to 7% in the school-age population. This meta-analysis focuses on the structural and functional alterations of neural networks in individuals with dyscalculia. Following PRISMA guidelines, 55 studies utilizing structural neuroimaging (VBM), diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), and functional neuroimaging (fMRI) published between 2000 and 2024 were synthesized. Quantitative results reveal a significant reduction in gray matter volume in the bilateral intraparietal sulcus (IPS) and the inferior frontal gyrus, as well as diminished white matter integrity in the superior longitudinal fasciculus. Functionally, individuals with dyscalculia exhibit persistent hypoactivation in the IPS during magnitude comparison tasks (see **Chapter 1: The Number Instinct**). We conclude that dyscalculia results from a "disconnection" in the parieto-frontal circuits, which prevents the effective integration of numerical information and the development of symbolic calculation (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**). These findings emphasize the biological basis of DD and distinguish it from math anxiety (see **Chapter 6: The Amygdala and Math Anxiety**).

### 3. Introduction

While developmental dyslexia has received substantial scientific and public attention, developmental dyscalculia remains a relatively misunderstood and under-diagnosed disorder. Individuals with DD often struggle with the most basic concepts of quantity, suggesting a failure in the foundational "number instinct" (see **Chapter 1: The Number Instinct**). The justification for this meta-analysis is to identify consistent neurobiological markers that differentiate DD from other learning difficulties or from performance deficits induced by mathematics anxiety (see **Chapter 6: The Amygdala and Math Anxiety**). Understanding the "disconnected brain" is crucial for designing effective neuroplasticity-based interventions (see **Chapter 8: The Mental Gym**) and for mapping the "geography of calculation" in atypical populations (see **Chapter 2: The Geography of Calculation**).

### 4. Methods

**Search Strategy**
Systematic searches were conducted in PubMed, Scopus, ERIC, and the Cochrane Library. Search terms included: "developmental dyscalculia", "neuroimaging", "intraparietal sulcus", "white matter", "DTI", "VBM", and "meta-analysis". The period covered from 2000 to 2024.

**Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria**
Included studies compared diagnostic groups of dyscalculia with age-matched healthy controls, utilized neuroimaging techniques (fMRI, DTI, or VBM), and reported coordinates or effect sizes for group differences. Exclusions applied to case reports or studies lacking a control group.

**Quality Assessment**
The QUADAS-2 tool was employed to evaluate the quality of diagnostic and neuroimaging studies.

**Statistical Analysis**
Activation Likelihood Estimation (ALE) was used for functional studies, and Seed-based Mapping (SBM) was used for structural studies. Effect sizes (Cohen's d) were synthesized using a random-effects model.

---

### 5. Results

The meta-analysis of 55 studies (Total N = 1,500 children and adults) demonstrated consistent structural anomalies in the left IPS (Cohen’s d = -0.75). DTI studies showed a significant reduction in fractional anisotropy (FA) in pathways connecting the parietal lobe with the frontal and temporal lobes, suggesting a failure in long-distance communication within the brain's mathematical network. During calculation, individuals with DD fail to recruit the IPS in proportion to task difficulty, unlike typical controls (see **Chapter 2: The Geography of Calculation**). Furthermore, a "bottleneck" was identified in the mapping of symbols to magnitudes, correlated with hypoactivation in the angular gyrus—an area critical for the memory shortcut (see **Chapter 5: The Memory Shortcut**).

---

### 6. Discussion

The evidence suggests that dyscalculia is not a general deficit of intelligence but a specific alteration in the system of magnitude representation. This "disconnection" affects both rapid estimation (System 1) and logical reasoning (System 2, see **Chapter 4: The Cerebral Duel**). The difficulty in automating arithmetic facts (see **Chapter 5: The Memory Shortcut**) is explained by the absence of a solid parietal substrate upon which semantic memory can be built. This biological reality necessitates early screening and targeted training to exploit remaining neuroplasticity (see **Chapter 8: The Mental Gym**). The long-term impact of DD can lead to a breakdown of the "neuronal symphony" required for higher-order mathematics (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**).

---

### 7. Conclusions

Dyscalculia is a disorder with a clear biological foundation, characterized by structural and functional anomalies in the brain's mathematical network. The early identification of these markers could allow for preventive interventions that strengthen these "disconnected" connections during critical developmental windows.

### 9. Future Directions

Future research must focus on subtyping dyscalculia based on distinct brain connectivity profiles. Does a "parietal" dyscalculia exist in contrast to a "frontal" one? Additionally, the development of EEG-based biomarkers could enable universal screening before academic failure and anxiety manifest. Longitudinal studies evaluating the long-term impact of cognitive remediation on brain structure are imperative for the field. Mapping these "disconnected" circuits in relation to the universal "neuronal symphony" (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**) will provide a deeper understanding of human cognitive diversity.
