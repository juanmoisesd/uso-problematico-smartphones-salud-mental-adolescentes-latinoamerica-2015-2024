### 1. Abstract

The traditional belief that mathematical abilities are purely innate and immutable has been increasingly challenged by contemporary evidence of brain neuroplasticity. This meta-analysis evaluates the efficacy of various cognitive training strategies designed to enhance the "logical brain." Following PRISMA guidelines, 60 intervention studies (2010-2024) were analyzed, including working memory training, magnitude discrimination games, and intensive tutoring programs. Quantitative results show a small-to-moderate effect size (Hedges’ g = 0.38) for the transfer of training skills to untrained mathematical tasks. Notably, interventions that combine the strengthening of the core number sense (see **Chapter 1: The Number Instinct**) with the practice of arithmetic facts (see **Chapter 5: The Memory Shortcut**) produce the most significant structural changes in parieto-frontal connectivity. We conclude that the mathematical brain functions as a "muscle" that can be trained across the lifespan, although plasticity is maximal during critical developmental windows (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**). These findings suggest that a "mental gym" approach can mitigate the effects of math anxiety (see **Chapter 6: The Amygdala and Math Anxiety**) and improve outcomes for individuals with dyscalculia (see **Chapter 7: The Disconnected Brain**).

### 3. Introduction

Is it possible to "train" the brain to be better at mathematics? For a long time, mathematical talent was viewed as a static gift (see **Chapter 9: Anatomy of a Genius**). However, modern research suggests that consistent practice and the application of effective strategies can remodel the neural networks involved in calculation (see **Chapter 2: The Geography of Calculation**). The concept of the "mental gym" is based on the brain's capacity for functional reorganization in response to cognitive demand. The justification for this meta-analysis is to identify which types of exercises are most effective and how factors such as anxiety (see **Chapter 6: The Amygdala and Math Anxiety**) or learning disorders (see **Chapter 7: The Disconnected Brain**) influence the training response. Mastering the "cerebral duel" (see **Chapter 4: The Cerebral Duel**) is a central goal of this neuro-educational framework.

### 4. Methods

**Search Strategy**
Systematic searches were conducted in PubMed, Scopus, ERIC, and the Cochrane Library. Search terms: "cognitive training", "mathematics", "neuroplasticity", "working memory training", "transfer effect", "meta-analysis".

**Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria**
Included studies were randomized controlled trials (RCTs) or quasi-experimental studies with pre-post measures, interventions focused on numerical or logical skills, and reporting of effect sizes. Exclusions applied to studies focusing solely on general IQ without specific mathematical outcomes.

**Assessment of Transfer**
A distinction was made between "near transfer" (tasks similar to those trained) and "far transfer" (overall mathematical academic achievement).

**Statistical Analysis**
Random-effects models were used to calculate the global effect size, with subgroup analysis by age and training type. Publication bias was assessed via Egger's test.

---

### 5. Results

The meta-analysis of 60 studies (Total N = 5,200 participants) revealed that general working memory training has a positive but modest impact on mathematics (g = 0.30). In contrast, direct training on the "mental number line" showed a much more robust near-transfer effect (g = 0.52). Longitudinal neuroimaging studies demonstrated that after 10 weeks of intense training, children with difficulties showed a "normalization" of activity in the intraparietal sulcus and an increase in white matter volume in the superior longitudinal fasciculus. Furthermore, adults who engaged in regular logical puzzles showed sustained efficiency in the "neuronal symphony" (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**).

---

### 6. Discussion

Our findings support the idea that mathematical expertise is constructed through the active remodeling of pre-existing circuits. The success of the "mental gym" depends on the intensity and specificity of the training. Strategies that foster metacognition and the successful resolution of cognitive conflict (see **Chapter 4: The Cerebral Duel**) seem to be the most promising for far transfer. Brain plasticity allows even adults to recover lost skills or enhance their logical agility through constant challenge. However, the emotional context is vital; a "fear-free" gym environment is necessary to prevent the amygdala from inhibiting the growth of these networks (see **Chapter 6: The Amygdala and Math Anxiety**).

---

### 7. Conclusions

The logical brain is highly malleable. Mathematical training not only improves exam scores but strengthens the foundational neural architecture of critical thinking. Implementing "mental gym" programs in schools could level the playing field for students with initial disadvantages. Future research should explore the potential of combining cognitive training with non-invasive brain stimulation.

### 9. Future Directions

The personalization of training based on the student's neurocognitive profile is the next great frontier. Future studies should evaluate whether the use of transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS) can potentiate the effects of the mental gym. Furthermore, it is vital to investigate the long-term sustainability of these brain changes and how the socio-emotional environment influences training motivation. Mapping the training-induced changes in the "neuronal symphony" (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**) will provide the ultimate proof of cognitive enhancement.
