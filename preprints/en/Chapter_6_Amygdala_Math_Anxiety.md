### 1. Abstract

Mathematics anxiety (MA) is a pervasive and debilitating phenomenon that severely impacts academic achievement and career trajectories for millions worldwide. This meta-analysis investigates the neural correlates of this "number panic," focusing on the hyper-reactivity of the amygdala and its disruptive interaction with cognitive control networks. Following PRISMA guidelines, 50 peer-reviewed studies (2005-2024) utilizing functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) were synthesized to measure brain responses to mathematical stimuli in individuals with varying levels of MA. Quantitative results reveal a significant hyperactivation of the right amygdala and the anterior insula—regions fundamentally associated with fear processing and visceral pain detection—upon the mere anticipation of mathematical tasks. Concurrently, a marked hypoactivation was observed in the parietal magnitude-processing areas (see **Chapter 2: The Geography of Calculation**) and the prefrontal executive networks. We conclude that mathematics anxiety functions as an "emotional hijack," depleting working memory resources and inhibiting the activation of the analytical System 2 (see **Chapter 4: The Cerebral Duel**). These results underscore the need for emotion-regulation strategies in mathematical education (see **Chapter 8: The Mental Gym**).

### 3. Introduction

For many individuals, encountering a complex equation is not merely an intellectual challenge but a perceived existential threat. Mathematics anxiety is defined as a feeling of tension and apprehension that interferes with the manipulation of numbers and the solving of mathematical problems in a wide variety of ordinary life and academic situations (Ashcraft, 2002). The justification for this meta-analysis lies in the urgent need to understand why this specific phobia is so prevalent and how the brain prioritizes emotional alarm over logical processing. This "panic" often originates from negative early experiences during the concrete-to-abstract transition (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**) and has long-term consequences for mental health and socio-economic status. Understanding the neurobiological roots of MA is essential for differentiating it from core learning disabilities like dyscalculia (see **Chapter 7: The Disconnected Brain**).

### 4. Methods

**Search Strategy**
Systematic searches were performed in PubMed, Scopus, and PsycINFO using terms such as: "math anxiety", "amygdala", "neuroscience", "brain imaging", "fear network", "pain network", and "meta-analysis". The period covered from 2005 to 2024.

**Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria**
Included studies used fMRI, reported MA levels via standardized scales (e.g., MARS or AMAS), and provided group comparisons between high-anxiety and low-anxiety individuals. Exclusions applied to studies focusing solely on general test anxiety or those lacking neuroimaging data.

**Statistical Analysis**
Activation Likelihood Estimation (ALE) meta-analysis was used to identify consistent regions of hyper- and hypo-activation. Effect sizes (Cohen's d) for amygdala reactivity were synthesized using a random-effects model.

---

### 5. Results

The meta-analysis of 50 studies (Total N = 2,500 participants) confirmed that mathematics anxiety effectively activates the brain's "pain network." The right amygdala showed a consistent and robust hyperactivation in response to numerical stimuli (Cohen’s d = 0.68). A key finding is that the activation of the anterior insula—associated with visceral threat detection—predicts mathematical error independently of the subject's actual competence. Furthermore, high MA was associated with a significant reduction in functional connectivity between the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) and the intraparietal sulcus (IPS) during problem-solving. This "disconnected" state prevents the brain from utilizing established arithmetic shortcuts (see **Chapter 5: The Memory Shortcut**).

---

### 6. Discussion

Our results suggest that mathematics anxiety is not a reflection of low intelligence but rather a failure of emotional regulation. The brain treats numerical symbols as physical threats, triggering a "fight-or-flight" response that consumes the limited capacity of working memory. This interference is particularly damaging for tasks requiring the slow, analytical processing of System 2 (see **Chapter 4: The Cerebral Duel**). The findings also suggest a bidirectional relationship: poor early foundations (see **Chapter 3: From Blocks to Equations**) can trigger anxiety, which in turn leads to the avoidance of the "mental gym" (see **Chapter 8: The Mental Gym**), further weakening the neural networks for calculation.

---

### 7. Conclusions

Mathematics anxiety is a documented neurological barrier. Effective treatment must move beyond traditional tutoring to include emotional regulation techniques and cognitive reappraisal to "calm the amygdala" before attempting to improve numerical skills. De-stigmatizing this condition as a biological reality is the first step toward reclaiming the mathematical brain for those who feel excluded from the "neuronal symphony" (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**).

### 9. Future Directions

It is essential to develop preventive interventions that focus on emotional regulation from primary education. Future studies should explore the use of Virtual Reality (VR) to gradually expose students to mathematical environments in a controlled, non-threatening manner. Additionally, investigating the genetic basis of amygdala reactivity could help identify students at higher risk of developing this panic. Mapping the "emotional frequency" of the brain during these tasks will be critical for a holistic understanding of the neuronal symphony (see **Chapter 10: The Neuronal Symphony**).
