## Abstract

**English:**
Mathematics anxiety (MA) is a pervasive affective-cognitive condition characterized by feelings of tension, apprehension, and fear that interfere with numerical manipulation and problem-solving. This study provides an extensive investigation into the neural correlates of MA, focusing on the hyperactivation of the amygdala and its downstream effects on the executive control network. Utilizing meta-analytic evidence from functional neuroimaging (fMRI), we demonstrate that in high-math-anxious individuals, the mere anticipation of a numerical task triggers activity in the posterior insula and mid-cingulate cortex—regions associated with the perception of visceral threat and physical pain. This emotional "hijacking" drains working memory resources from the prefrontal cortex, leading to a significant decrease in performance that is independent of actual mathematical ability. We also discuss the role of the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex in successful emotion regulation and the potential for interventions such as cognitive reappraisal, expressive writing, and inhibitory control training to mitigate the "number panic."

## Introduction
For many individuals, an equation is not a logical challenge but a source of visceral terror. Mathematics anxiety (MA) affects a significant portion of the global population, with profound consequences for career choices, economic stability, and psychological well-being (Ashcraft, 2002). MA is distinct from general test anxiety or low intelligence; it is a specific, maladaptive emotional response that blocks the brain's calculating capacity.

The objective of this article is to unravel the neurobiology of MA, analyzing how the brain's emotional circuitry can compromise logical processing and exploring evidence-based strategies for remediation.

## Theoretical Framework
### The Pain and Fear Networks
Pioneering research by Lyons and Beilock (2012) revealed that the anticipation of math activates the "pain matrix" of the brain, including the bilateral dorso-posterior insula. Simultaneously, individuals with high MA show hyperactivation in the right amygdala—the brain's fear center—during numerical tasks (Young et al., 2012). This suggest that for these individuals, math is processed as a threat to physical or social integrity.

### Cognitive Interference and Working Memory
The "distraction model" of MA posits that anxious thoughts (worry) act as a secondary task that competes for the limited resources of working memory. Since mathematical problem-solving is highly demanding of working memory (see Chapter 3), this competition leads to errors and slower processing (Beilock & Willingham, 2014; de la Serna, 2025e).

### Neural Predictors of Resilience
Interestingly, not all individuals with high MA perform poorly. Those who succeed show increased functional connectivity between the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC) and the amygdala, suggesting that they are effectively "braking" their emotional response through top-down cognitive control (Supekar et al., 2015).

## Methodology
This study comprises a systematic review of affective neuroscience literature published between 2010 and 2025, including 110 fMRI studies and behavioral experiments on emotion regulation in mathematical contexts.

## Discussion
MA represents a failure of the integration between affect and cognition. The hyperactive amygdala not only creates distress but actively inhibits the intraparietal sulcus and the dlPFC—the core of the "calculating brain" (see Chapter 2). This creates a vicious cycle: anxiety leads to failure, and failure reinforces the belief that math is threatening.

Furthermore, MA is socially contagious. Studies have shown that a teacher's or parent's anxiety can be transmitted to children, leading to early developmental changes in the child's neural response to numbers (Maloney & Beilock, 2012). Addressing MA requires a two-pronged approach: reducing the amygdala's reactivity (affective) and strengthening the prefrontal-parietal integrity (cognitive).

## Conclusions
Understanding MA as a real neural phenomenon—rather than a lack of "talent"—is the first step toward more humane and effective education. Interventions should incorporate psychological support to de-sensitize the fear response, allowing the calculating brain to function at its full potential.

## Future Directions
Future research should explore the use of virtual reality (VR) for exposure therapy in math-anxious students and the impact of mindfulness-based stress reduction on the connectivity between the amygdala and the prefrontal cortex.
