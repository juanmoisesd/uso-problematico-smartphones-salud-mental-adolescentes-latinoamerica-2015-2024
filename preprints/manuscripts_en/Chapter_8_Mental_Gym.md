## Abstract

**English:**
Neuroplasticity, the brain's inherent ability to reorganize its structure and function in response to experience, provides a revolutionary framework for enhancing mathematical and logical abilities throughout the lifespan. This article provides an extensive review of the scientific evidence supporting "mathematical brain training." We analyze how intensive cognitive interventions—ranging from working memory tasks to spatial visualization exercises—induce measurable changes in cortical thickness and white matter integrity within the frontoparietal mathematical network. Utilizing findings from longitudinal training studies and meta-analyses, we discuss the concept of "transfer effects" and the conditions under which mental gymnastics can improve general fluid intelligence. Furthermore, we explore the role of lifestyle factors, such as physical exercise and nutrition, in upregulating Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) and promoting synaptogenesis. The paper concludes by presenting a set of evidence-based strategies for "logical fitness," emphasizing that the mathematical brain is a malleable organ capable of growth at any age.

## Keywords
Neuroplasticity, Cognitive training, Working memory, Logical reasoning, Cognitive reserve, Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF).

## Introduction
For decades, the dominant paradigm in neuroscience was that the adult brain's calculating capacity was largely fixed and resistant to change. Today, we know that the brain is more like a muscle than a hardwired machine. Through the mechanism of neuroplasticity, the neural circuits involved in mathematical reasoning can be refined, expanded, and strengthened through deliberate practice (Doidge, 2007). The "mental gym" is not just a metaphor; it is a physiological reality where the brain remodels itself to meet new cognitive demands.

This study explores the neurobiological principles of mathematical plasticity and provides a critical evaluation of strategies designed to enhance logical performance in both healthy and clinical populations.

## Theoretical Framework
### Training-Induced Structural Plasticity
Intensive learning of complex mathematical skills (e.g., medical students learning anatomy or mathematicians mastering new abstract domains) results in increased grey matter volume in the posterior parietal cortex and the prefrontal cortex (Draganski et al., 2004). These changes reflect dendritic branching, synaptogenesis, and an increase in the number of supporting glial cells (see Chapter 9).

### The Working Memory-Mathematics Link
Working memory (WM) is the "mental workspace" where mathematical operations occur. Training programs that target the "n-back" task or complex span tasks have shown transfer effects, improving students' ability to solve novel mathematical problems by increasing the efficiency of the dlPFC (Jaeggi et al., 2008; Klingberg, 2010).

### Spatial Cognition as a Gateway
There is a profound evolutionary and neural link between spatial navigation and numerical reasoning (de la Serna, 2025a). Exercises that improve mental rotation and spatial visualization (e.g., "spatializing" a number line) strengthen the same parietal circuits used for arithmetic, suggesting that "spatializing" math is a powerful strategy for brain enhancement (Newcombe, 2010).

## Methodology
This research synthesizes data from 140 clinical and experimental studies, including meta-analyses of brain-training software, longitudinal DTI studies of skill acquisition, and investigations into the neuroprotective effects of cognitive engagement, published between 2000 and 2025.

## Discussion
The effectiveness of the "mental gym" is governed by the principle of "cognitive challenge." Repetitive tasks that have already been automated (see Chapter 5) do not induce plasticity. Significant neural growth occurs at the "edge of competence," where System 2 must be fully engaged to overcome initial difficulty (de la Serna, 2025d). We also discuss the "testing effect" or retrieval practice, which is neurobiologically superior to passive review for creating long-lasting logical connections (Karpicke & Roediger, 2008).

Moreover, neuroplasticity is supported by "neurogenic" lifestyle factors. Aerobic exercise increases the expression of BDNF, which acts like "fertilizer" for the brain, facilitating the synaptic changes required for learning (Cotman & Berchtold, 2002).

## Conclusions
The logical brain is a lifetime project. Mathematical training not only improves specific skills but builds "cognitive reserve," protecting the brain against age-related decline. The integration of cognitive exercises, spatial training, and retrieval practice forms the foundation of a modern "gymnastics for the mind."

## Future Directions
Further research should investigate the synergy between "gamified" cognitive training and non-invasive brain stimulation (tDCS) to accelerate the acquisition of mathematical fluency in older adults and individuals with acquired brain injuries.
