## Abstract

**English:**
This study delineates the neuroanatomical and functional organization of mathematical cognition within the human brain. Utilizing advanced functional neuroimaging techniques (fMRI, PET, DTI), we map a distributed neural network that integrates the parietal cortex, frontal lobe, and occipito-temporal regions. The bilateral intraparietal sulcus (IPS) is identified as the core substrate for magnitude representation and approximate calculation. In contrast, the left angular gyrus is primarily recruited for the retrieval of overlearned arithmetic facts, such as multiplication tables, mediated by verbal memory. The prefrontal cortex, specifically the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC), manages executive control, working memory, and algorithmic strategy selection. We also examine the structural connectivity provided by the superior longitudinal fasciculus and its role in mathematical proficiency. The results highlight a dynamic transition from frontal to parietal reliance as mathematical expertise develops, providing a comprehensive "geography" of the calculating brain.

## Introduction
The ability to perform mathematical calculations is among the most complex cognitive functions of the human species. It does not reside in a single "mathematical center" but emerges from the coordinated interaction of multiple, specialized brain regions (Arsalidou & Taylor, 2011). Understanding the cerebral geography of calculation allows us not only to comprehend the normal workings of the mind but also to address developmental and acquired disorders such as acalculia and dyscalculia.

This study aims to provide a high-resolution map of the areas activated during numerical processing and arithmetic operations, analyzing the functional specialization of each node within the mathematical network. We move beyond simple localization to discuss the connectivity and dynamics of these systems.

## Theoretical Framework
### The Triple-Code Model
Proposed by Dehaene (1992, 2003), this model remains the cornerstone of numerical neuroscience. It posits three distinct numerical representations:
1.  **Analog Magnitude Code:** Located in the bilateral IPS, used for estimating and comparing quantities.
2.  **Verbal Code:** Located in the left angular gyrus and perisylvian areas, used for language-based arithmetic retrieval.
3.  **Visual Arabic Code:** Located in the fusiform gyrus (visual number form area), used for recognizing numerals.

### The Role of the Posterior Parietal Cortex
The IPS is universally recognized as the core of number sense (de la Serna, 2025a). Functional MRI studies show that its activation is proportional to the difficulty of magnitude comparison—the "numerical distance effect" (Pinel et al., 2001).

### Frontoparietal Interaction
Active calculation requires the recruitment of the prefrontal cortex for executive functions. The dlPFC is critical for maintaining intermediate results in working memory and for the sequential application of arithmetic rules (Amalric & Dehaene, 2016).

## Methodology
A qualitative meta-analysis was performed on functional neuroimaging studies published between 2005 and 2025. We selected investigations that utilized mental arithmetic tasks, number-to-space mapping, and complex algebraic problem-solving in both healthy adults and experts. Data on structural connectivity (DTI) were also integrated to map the "white matter geography" of calculation.

## Results and Discussion
Our findings confirm that addition and subtraction rely heavily on the bilateral activation of the IPS. In contrast, multiplication shows a marked left-hemisphere bias, specifically involving the angular gyrus, reflecting its dependence on rote verbal memory (Grabner et al., 2009). The frontal cortex (BA 9, 46) is consistently activated in tasks requiring planning and error monitoring.

Furthermore, expertise in mathematics is associated with a "frontal-to-parietal shift." Novices rely more on frontal areas for effortful processing (see Chapter 4), while experts show more efficient, automated parietal activity and increased integrity in the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF), the primary highway connecting the calculating nodes (Ansari, 2008).

## Conclusions
The "geography of calculation" is both dynamic and hierarchical. While magnitude foundations are parietal, complexity recruits frontal and temporal support networks. The structural integrity of these connections is as important as the health of the cortical nodes themselves. This map provides a framework for future interventions in neuroeducation and neuropsychology.

## Future Directions
Further research should explore the role of subcortical structures (basal ganglia and cerebellum) in the automation of mathematical processes and the use of Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) to modulate activity in the IPS for clinical purposes.
