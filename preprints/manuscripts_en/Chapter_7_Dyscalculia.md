## Abstract

**English:**
Developmental dyscalculia (DD) is a specific learning disorder characterized by persistent difficulties in acquiring basic arithmetic skills, despite average intelligence and adequate educational opportunities. This article provides an extensive neurobiological review of DD, focusing on the structural and functional abnormalities of the intraparietal sulcus (IPS) and its connectivity with the prefrontal and temporal cortices. Utilizing evidence from voxel-based morphometry (VBM) and diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), we demonstrate that individuals with DD exhibit reduced grey matter density in the IPS and lower white matter integrity in the superior longitudinal fasciculus. These "disconnections" in the neural network for calculation impair the fluid processing of numerical magnitudes and symbols. We also discuss the "number sense deficit" hypothesis versus the "access deficit" hypothesis and analyze how compensatory mechanisms in the frontal lobes lead to increased cognitive effort and fatigue. The paper concludes with an empathetic and scientific perspective on early diagnosis and the design of neuro-rehabilitative interventions.

## Introduction
To a child with developmental dyscalculia (DD), the world of numbers is an alien and confusing territory. What others perceive as a natural intuition (e.g., instantly knowing that 9 is greater than 4), they experience as a source of cognitive exhaustion and frustration. DD affects between 3% and 7% of the school-age population—a prevalence comparable to dyslexia—yet it has historically received significantly less research funding and clinical attention (Butterworth, 2010).

The objective of this study is to elucidate the neurobiological foundations of DD, analyzing why certain neural networks fail to integrate correctly during development and how these biological differences manifest as behavioral challenges in the classroom.

## Theoretical Framework
### The Number Sense Deficit Hypothesis
The dominant theory in the field suggests that DD arises from a primary dysfunction in the core system for analog magnitude representation, located in the bilateral IPS (Piazza et al., 2010; de la Serna, 2025a). Without a solid foundation in non-symbolic quantity estimation, the construction of higher-level mathematical concepts becomes precarious.

### The Access Deficit Hypothesis
An alternative view argues that the core of DD is not a problem with magnitude processing per se, but a failure in the connection between symbols (Arabic numerals) and the magnitudes they represent. This "disconnection" between the visual-symbolic areas and the parietal magnitude nodes prevents the efficient mapping required for calculation (Rousselle & Noël, 2007).

### Neuroanatomical Evidence
High-resolution neuroimaging has revealed that DD is associated with structural alterations. VBM studies show reduced grey matter in the right IPS, and DTI studies indicate impaired connectivity in the fasciculus longitudinalis superior, which links the parietal "calculating" nodes with the frontal "executive" nodes (Ayyıldız et al., 2021). This suggests that DD is a network-level disorder rather than a localized lesion.

## Methodology
This research synthesizes findings from 130 scientific publications, including structural and functional neuroimaging studies, longitudinal behavioral assessments, and meta-analyses of dyscalculia interventions published between 2005 and 2025.

## Discussion
DD is not the result of laziness or low general intelligence. During arithmetic tasks, the dyscalculic brain typically shows hypoactivation in the IPS and a compensatory hyperactivation in the prefrontal cortex (Price et al., 2007). This "frontal-heavy" processing explains why children with DD tire more quickly and struggle with multi-step problems; they are using effortful System 2 resources for tasks that should be handled by automated System 1 circuits (see Chapter 4).

Comorbidity with ADHD and dyslexia is common, suggesting shared neuro-developmental vulnerabilities. Early detection through "non-symbolic comparison" tasks (e.g., comparing groups of dots) is crucial, as early interventions utilizing brain plasticity can help normalize IPS activity before the gap between the student and their peers becomes insurmountable (Iuculano et al., 2015).

## Conclusions
Dyscalculia is a clinical manifestation of a "disconnected" mathematical brain. Understanding its neurobiological roots is essential for eliminating the stigma associated with the disorder and for developing specialized pedagogical tools that leverage alternative neural pathways (e.g., using multisensory and visuospatial cues) to teach numerical concepts.

## Future Directions
Future research should focus on the genetic and epigenetic factors that regulate the development of parietal circuits and explore the efficacy of non-invasive brain stimulation (tDCS) as an adjunct to intensive cognitive training in children with severe DD.
