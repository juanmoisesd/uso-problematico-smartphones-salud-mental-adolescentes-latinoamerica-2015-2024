## Abstract

**English:**
This study explores the intricate interaction between System 1 (fast, intuitive, and heuristic) and System 2 (slow, analytical, and deliberate) in the context of mathematical reasoning. Grounded in Dual-Process Theory, we analyze how cognitive biases and initial "gut feelings" can either facilitate or obstruct formal logical-formal performance. Neuroimaging evidence suggests that System 1 recruits areas associated with automatic processing and associative memory, such as the basal ganglia and ventromedial prefrontal cortex. In contrast, System 2 heavily activates the frontoparietal executive control network. A critical focus of this paper is the role of inhibitory control as the neural mechanism that allows System 2 to "override" erroneous System 1 responses. We discuss the developmental trajectory of these systems and how mathematical expertise transforms once-effortful System 2 processes into automated System 1 heuristics. Implications for education suggest that fostering "metacognitive monitoring" is as crucial as teaching algorithmic procedures.

## Introduction
Solving a mathematical problem is rarely a purely linear, logical act. Instead, it is often a silent conflict between what "looks" right at first glance and what the rigorous application of rules dictates. This "cerebral duel" was popularized by Daniel Kahneman (2011) through the distinction between System 1 (fast thinking) and System 2 (slow thinking). In mathematics, System 1 handles the innate number sense (de la Serna, 2025a) and learned shortcuts, while System 2 is responsible for hypothetical-deductive reasoning and the manipulation of abstract variables (de la Serna, 2025c).

This article aims to analyze the neurobiological foundations of this duality and explore how the interplay between intuition and logic determines mathematical competence across the lifespan.

## Theoretical Framework
### Dual-Process Theory in Mathematics
System 1 is characterized by being automatic, effortless, and often unconscious. It relies on associative memory and heuristics (e.g., the "larger number is more" heuristic). System 2 is controlled, effortful, and rule-based. Mathematical proficiency requires a high degree of "cognitive decoupling," where the mind temporarily ignores intuitive representations to simulate logical operations (Evans & Stanovich, 2013).

### The Neural Mechanism of Inhibition
The ability to inhibit a compelling but incorrect intuition is the hallmark of a successful mathematician. This function is mediated by the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) for conflict detection and the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) for the implementation of the logical rule (Gronchi & Giovannelli, 2024). Studies using the "bat and ball" problem have shown that even when individuals give the wrong answer, their ACC shows activation, suggesting that the brain "knows" there is a conflict even if System 2 fails to intervene (De Neys, 2012).

### Neuroanatomy of Reasoning
Functional MRI data reveal that as problems move from intuitive to analytical, there is a shift in activity from the posterior "sensory" regions to the medial and lateral prefrontal cortex (Houdé & Borst, 2015). This transition is especially evident during the learning of counter-intuitive concepts like negative numbers or fractions.

## Methodology
This study reviews experimental literature combining behavioral metrics (response times, error patterns) with neuroimaging data (fMRI, EEG) from tasks designed to elicit conflict between intuition and logic, involving over 2,000 participants across 45 studies.

## Discussion
Intuition (System 1) is not the enemy of mathematics; rather, it is the foundation of expert "insight." Professional mathematicians often "see" a solution before they can prove it. However, for the novice, System 1 is a frequent source of "systematic errors." A key goal of mathematics education should be to refine System 1 through extensive practice (see Chapter 5) so that accurate mathematical facts become part of the intuitive repertoire.

Furthermore, we explore how factors like time pressure and anxiety (see Chapter 6) selectively impair System 2, forcing the individual to rely on a System 1 that may be ill-equipped for the task.

## Conclusions
Mathematical excellence is defined by a harmonious dance between intuition and logic. Success depends on the strength of the inhibitory control mechanism and the richness of the automated knowledge base. Educators should emphasize "meta-reasoning"—teaching students to recognize the "traps" set by their own System 1.

## Future Directions
Future research should investigate how "nudges" in instructional design can help students engage System 2 more effectively and how individual differences in "need for cognition" relate to neural efficiency during reasoning.
