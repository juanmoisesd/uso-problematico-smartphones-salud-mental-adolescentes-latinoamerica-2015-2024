## Abstract

**English:**
The transition from effortful algorithmic problem-solving to the automatic retrieval of arithmetic facts from long-term memory is a critical milestone in the development of mathematical expertise. This article provides an extensive neurobiological analysis of this automation process. We examine the functional shift from the hippocampal scaffolding system to the cortical representation nodes, specifically the left angular gyrus and the posterior parietal cortex. Through a synthesis of longitudinal neuroimaging data, we discuss how intensive mathematical practice induces structural plasticity, including increased white matter integrity in the superior longitudinal fasciculus and the arcuate fasciculus. This "memory shortcut" reduces the cognitive load on the prefrontal working memory system, liberating resources for higher-order reasoning and complex problem-solving. We also explore the role of sleep and spacing effects in the consolidation of mathematical formulas and theorems, providing an evidence-based framework for optimizing mathematical fluency.

## Introduction
How do we know that 8 times 7 is 56 without performing a mental calculation? This phenomenon is known as arithmetic retrieval or automation. The ability to store and instantly access mathematical facts is what allows the human brain to tackle advanced engineering, physics, and cryptography (Ashcraft, 1992). Without these "memory shortcuts," the cognitive system would be overwhelmed by the computational demands of basic operations, leaving no room for the System 2 logic required for innovation (de la Serna, 2025d).

This study explores the neural architecture that supports mathematical memory, tracing the journey from the initial encoding of a rule to its lifelong consolidation as an automated heuristic.

## Theoretical Framework
### From Calculation to Retrieval: The Strategy Shift
Early in learning, children rely on procedural strategies (e.g., finger counting or decomposition), which activate the prefrontal cortex and the intraparietal sulcus (IPS). Proficiency is marked by a shift to direct retrieval, which bypasses the IPS and recruits the left angular gyrus (AG)—an area associated with verbal semantics and rote memory (Grabner et al., 2009; de la Serna, 2025b).

### The Hippocampal Scaffolding
The hippocampus acts as a temporary "index" during the acquisition of new mathematical associations. Longitudinal studies by Menon and colleagues (Qin et al., 2014) demonstrate that as children mature, the hippocampus becomes increasingly connected to the neocortex, eventually transferring the "ownership" of the mathematical facts to the angular gyrus and temporal lobes.

### Structural Basis of Fluency
Mathematical expertise is not just about "firing" neurons but about the "wiring" between them. High-performing students show greater fractional anisotropy (FA)—a measure of white matter integrity—in the fasciculus longitudinalis superior, which connects the parietal calculating centers with the frontal executive centers (Vestergren et al., 2015).

## Methodology
This research integrates data from 85 neuroimaging studies (fMRI and DTI) and cognitive psychology experiments on the "spacing effect" and "testing effect" in mathematical contexts, covering a range of participants from primary school students to professional mathematicians.

## Discussion
Automation is not rote memorization in a vacuum; it is the creation of a rich numerical semantic network. The left angular gyrus serves as a hub for facts that have been verbalized (multiplication tables), while subtraction and division often remain more procedural and dependent on the IPS (Dehaene, 2011). In cases of acalculia, damage to the AG can leave the patient able to compare magnitudes but unable to state that 2+2=4.

We also examine the impact of sleep on "offline consolidation." Emerging evidence suggests that mathematical insights and the stability of learned formulas are significantly enhanced by post-learning sleep, which facilitates the reorganization of hippocampal-cortical circuits (Wagner et al., 2004).

## Conclusions
The calculating brain is a dynamic system striving for maximum efficiency. The automation of foundational mathematics is a prerequisite for advanced logical thought. Educational strategies should balance conceptual understanding with the spaced practice necessary for neural consolidation.

## Future Directions
Future research should investigate the use of neurofeedback to help students monitor their own "retrieval state" and the development of AI-driven personalized learning paths that optimize the timing of practice to match individual consolidation rates.
