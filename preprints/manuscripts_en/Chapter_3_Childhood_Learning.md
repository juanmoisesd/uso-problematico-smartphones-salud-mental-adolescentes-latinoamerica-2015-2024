## Abstract

**English:**
The development of mathematical thinking in childhood represents one of the most complex cognitive transformations in human ontogeny: the transition from the biological perception of physical magnitudes to the manipulation of abstract, symbolic concepts. This article provides an extensive review of the neuroscientific evidence mapping this developmental trajectory. We analyze the role of "concreteness fading" as an instructional strategy that aligns with the brain's natural tendency to build abstract schemas upon concrete sensory-motor foundations. Longitudinal neuroimaging data reveal that formal instruction triggers a functional reorganization, shifting neural activity from a primitive, non-symbolic system to a specialized frontoparietal network. Furthermore, we discuss the critical contribution of domain-general functions, such as working memory, inhibitory control, and visuospatial processing, as predictors of algebraic readiness. The paper concludes by emphasizing the importance of neuroeducation in designing curricula that respect the maturation timelines of these neural circuits.

## Introduction
The acquisition of mathematical proficiency is not a linear accumulation of facts but a profound reconfiguration of pre-existing neural circuits. While infants are born with an innate "number sense" (de la Serna, 2025a), the leap to symbolic mathematics requires the brain to "recycle" areas originally evolved for other purposes, such as language and object recognition (Dehaene, 2011). This transition from the concrete (e.g., counting blocks or fingers) to the abstract (e.g., manipulating variables in an equation) is a defining milestone of human cognitive development.

The objective of this manuscript is to examine the neurobiological mechanisms underlying the acquisition of abstract mathematical concepts during childhood and early adolescence, exploring how pedagogical interventions can optimize this process through the lens of brain plasticity.

## Theoretical Framework
### The Mapping of Symbols to Magnitudes
The "mapping hypothesis" posits that children learn the meaning of number symbols (e.g., the numeral "5") by linking them to pre-existing non-symbolic representations of magnitude in the intraparietal sulcus (Siegler & Lortie-Forgues, 2014). This mapping is essential for the development of the "mental number line," a spatial representation of numbers that grows in precision with age and instruction.

### Concreteness Fading and the CRA Sequence
The Concrete-Representational-Abstract (CRA) sequence is an instructional model supported by neurocognitive research. It suggests that learning is most robust when it follows a three-stage progression:
1.  **Concrete:** Direct manipulation of physical objects (activating motor and somatosensory areas).
2.  **Representational:** Use of pictorial representations (engaging visual-spatial networks).
3.  **Abstract:** Transition to purely symbolic notation (recruiting specialized frontoparietal circuits).
This "concreteness fading" allows for the gradual extraction of structural properties while shedding unnecessary surface details (Fyfe et al., 2014).

### The Role of Domain-General Executive Functions
Mathematical success is heavily dependent on the maturation of the prefrontal cortex. Working memory allows for the maintenance of intermediate results, while inhibitory control is necessary to suppress intuitive but incorrect responses (e.g., the belief that 1/4 is larger than 1/2 because 4 is larger than 2) (Blair & Razza, 2007; de la Serna, 2025d).

## Methodology
This study synthesizes findings from over 120 peer-reviewed articles, including longitudinal fMRI studies, behavioral experiments on instructional design, and meta-analyses of mathematical learning interventions published between 2010 and 2025.

## Discussion
Neuroimaging evidence shows that as children gain mathematical expertise, there is a "parietalization" of function—a shift from heavy reliance on the prefrontal cortex (reflecting effortful, procedural processing) to the posterior parietal cortex (reflecting automated, semantic processing) (Ansari, 2016). However, the transition to high-level algebra in adolescence introduces new challenges, requiring the brain to treat operations themselves as objects of thought. This "second-order abstraction" relies on the integrity of white matter tracts, such as the arcuate fasciculus, which facilitates the integration of verbal and numerical codes (Dumontheil & Klingberg, 2012).

The persistence of math-related difficulties often stems from a failure to consolidate these symbolic-to-magnitude mappings during the early school years, leading to a cascade of delays as the curriculum becomes increasingly abstract.

## Conclusions
Childhood mathematical learning is a process of refined neural recycling and network specialization. The use of concrete mediators is not merely a pedagogical convenience but a biological necessity for anchoring abstract concepts. Neuroeducation offers a powerful framework for aligning teaching methods with the developmental trajectory of the calculating brain.

## Future Directions
Research should investigate the impact of digital learning environments and gamified mathematics on the formation of abstract representations, specifically looking at how interactive simulations might accelerate the "concreteness fading" process.
